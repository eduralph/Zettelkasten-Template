Author: Gerolamo Cardano

# Description
Written in 1576 (the year of his death) and published posthumously in 1643, Cardano's autobiography — *The Book of My Life* — is considered one of the first modern psychological autobiographies. It is famous for being incredibly raw, honest, and bizarre. Instead of just listing his achievements, Cardano dedicated entire chapters to his deepest flaws, oddest habits, and personal tragedies.

In the book, he writes extensively about:

- His severe gambling addiction (which paradoxically led him to write the first systematic book on the mathematics of probability).
- His eccentricities, health anxieties, and belief in a guardian spirit.
- The tragic execution of his eldest son for murdering his wife.
- His work as a renowned physician across Europe.

# Historical Context

Cardano (1501–1576) lived through the height of the Italian Renaissance, an era when the boundaries between mathematician, physician, astrologer, philosopher, and mystic were porous. He was born illegitimately in Pavia, fought against social stigma his entire life, and rose to become one of the most famous polymaths of his age — only to fall into disgrace late in life when he was imprisoned by the Inquisition for casting a horoscope of Jesus Christ.

The autobiography was written in the final, lonely years of his life, after the catastrophic loss of his son Giovanni Battista. It reads as both confession and accounting — a man taking inventory of himself before death.

# Structure of the Book

The book is unusual: rather than narrating his life chronologically, Cardano organizes it into ~54 short thematic chapters, each examining a single facet of himself. Topics include:

- His birth, parents, and education
- His physical appearance and health
- His virtues — and at greater length, his vices
- His dreams and the "guardian spirit" he believed protected him
- His enemies, friends, and patrons
- His honors, books, and intellectual achievements
- His son's execution and his own imprisonment
- His gambling, eating habits, and sexual life

This thematic-confessional structure prefigures modern memoir and psychological self-examination by centuries.

# Key Themes

### 1. Radical Self-Examination
Cardano dissects himself with the same cold curiosity he brings to mathematics. He catalogs his stutter, his insomnia, his impotence in early adulthood, his cruelty, his vanity. Few autobiographies before — or even since — match this willingness to expose oneself.

### 2. Fortune and Chance
Having been a compulsive gambler for decades, Cardano was obsessed with *Fortuna*. His *[[Liber de Ludo Aleae]]* (Book on Games of Chance) — written earlier but published posthumously — is the first systematic treatment of probability. The autobiography returns repeatedly to the role of luck, fate, and divine providence in shaping his life.

### 3. The Wounded Healer
Despite being one of Europe's most sought-after physicians (he was once summoned to treat the Archbishop of Scotland), Cardano was a deeply troubled man — wracked by illness, depression, and grief. The book is a portrait of a healer who could not heal himself.

### 4. The Daimon / Guardian Spirit
Like Socrates, Cardano claimed to be guided by a personal *daimon* — a guardian spirit that warned and counseled him. He treats this not as metaphor but as literal fact, blending the rational and the mystical in a way characteristic of the late Renaissance.

### 5. Tragedy and Endurance
The execution of his beloved son Giovanni Battista in 1560 broke Cardano. He returns to this wound throughout the book. The autobiography is partly an attempt to make sense of suffering — to find meaning in a life that contained both extraordinary achievement and unbearable loss.

# Why It Matters

- **Birth of modern autobiography**: Predates Rousseau's *Confessions* by nearly 200 years, yet anticipates its psychological honesty.
- **Window into Renaissance mentality**: Shows how a brilliant 16th-century mind held mathematics, medicine, astrology, dream interpretation, and mystical experience as parts of a single coherent worldview.
- **Humanizing portrait of a genius**: Cardano contributed foundational work to algebra (the [[Cardano formula]] for cubic equations), probability, medicine, cryptography, and mechanics — yet the book reveals a fragile, flawed, suffering human being behind those achievements.

# Cardano's Other Major Works

- *[[Ars Magna]]* (1545) — landmark algebra text, first published solutions to the general cubic and quartic equations. Center of the famous priority dispute with [[Niccolò Tartaglia]].
- *[[Liber de Ludo Aleae]]* — first systematic treatise on probability, written from a gambler's perspective.
- *De Subtilitate* (1550) — encyclopedic natural philosophy.
- *De Rerum Varietate* (1557) — companion volume on the variety of natural phenomena.
